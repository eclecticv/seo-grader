# E-E-A-T Guidelines by Content Type

How to embed Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness signals into each content brief. Every brief should include specific E-E-A-T recommendations tailored to the content format and topic.

## The Four Signals

### Experience
Demonstrate first-hand experience with the topic.
- Personal anecdotes, case results, screenshots of real usage
- "We tested X for 6 months and here's what happened"
- Before/after data from actual implementation
- Photos, screenshots, or videos showing real usage

### Expertise
Show deep knowledge of the subject matter.
- Author has credentials or background relevant to the topic
- Content covers nuances that only an expert would know
- Technical accuracy, correct terminology
- Cites primary sources (not just other blog posts)

### Authoritativeness
External validation of the author/site's authority.
- Author bio with relevant credentials, links to LinkedIn/portfolio
- Site has been cited by other authoritative sources
- Published in or featured by industry publications
- Customer logos, testimonials, case studies on the site

### Trustworthiness
Signals that the content and site are reliable.
- Clear author attribution (not "admin" or anonymous)
- Transparent about methodology ("How we tested", "Our criteria")
- Updated date visible
- Disclosures (affiliate links, sponsored content, conflicts of interest)
- HTTPS, privacy policy, clear contact information

## E-E-A-T by Content Format

### Comparison Posts ("[X] vs [Y]")
| Signal | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Experience | Include screenshots of both products in use. Mention how long you've tested each. |
| Expertise | Explain evaluation criteria and why each matters for the target audience. |
| Authority | Link to detailed reviews or case studies for each product. |
| Trust | Disclose any affiliate relationships. Show a comparison methodology section. |

### How-To Guides
| Signal | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Experience | Show real results from following the guide. Include your own screenshots/examples. |
| Expertise | Author should have demonstrable experience doing the thing. Include an author bio. |
| Authority | Link to official documentation or primary sources for technical claims. |
| Trust | Include a "Prerequisites" or "Who this is for" section. Update dates regularly. |

### Listicles ("Best [X] for [Y]")
| Signal | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Experience | Indicate which items you've personally tested. Flag "hands-on tested" items. |
| Expertise | Explain selection criteria upfront. Show why these specific items made the list. |
| Authority | Include ratings from authoritative review sources (G2, Capterra, etc.). |
| Trust | Methodology section explaining how you chose and ranked items. Update frequency. |

### Thought Leadership / Guides
| Signal | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Experience | Draw on specific company experiences, client results, industry observations. |
| Expertise | Deep, original analysis — not rephrasing what others have said. Unique data or frameworks. |
| Authority | Author should be named, with bio showing relevant credentials. |
| Trust | Cite data sources. Acknowledge limitations or opposing viewpoints. |

### Case Studies
| Signal | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Experience | The entire piece IS an experience signal. Include specific metrics, timelines, challenges. |
| Expertise | Explain WHY decisions were made, not just what was done. Show strategic thinking. |
| Authority | Name the client (with permission) or describe the industry/size clearly. |
| Trust | Include before/after metrics. Be honest about what didn't work. |

### Product/Category Pages (for ecommerce)
| Signal | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| Experience | Staff picks, "our team's favorite", editor's notes from product testers. |
| Expertise | Detailed specifications, technical explanations, buying guidance. |
| Authority | Manufacturer partnerships, certifications, awards. |
| Trust | Clear return policies, customer reviews, transparent pricing. |

## E-E-A-T Brief Format

For each content brief, include an E-E-A-T section structured as:

```
### E-E-A-T Signals
- **Experience**: [Specific recommendation for this post — what firsthand experience to demonstrate]
- **Expertise**: [What expertise signals to include — author qualifications, depth indicators]
- **Authority**: [How to establish authority — citations, credentials, external validation]
- **Trust**: [Trust signals — methodology, disclosures, transparency elements]
```

## Topic Sensitivity (YMYL)

For Your Money or Your Life topics (finance, health, legal, safety), E-E-A-T requirements are significantly higher:

- **Must have** credentialed author (CPA for finance, MD for health, JD for legal)
- **Must cite** primary sources (studies, official guidelines, regulations)
- **Must include** disclaimers ("This is not financial/medical/legal advice")
- **Must update** regularly with visible last-updated date

If the startup's content strategy touches YMYL topics, flag this in the report with specific credential requirements.

## Common E-E-A-T Failures to Avoid

1. **Ghost authors**: Never use "Admin", "Team", or anonymous bylines. Every post needs a real author.
2. **No author bio**: An author page with photo, bio, credentials, and links to other work is essential.
3. **Recycled content**: Restating what the top 5 Google results already say adds no E-E-A-T. Original perspective is required.
4. **No methodology**: For comparison/review content, always explain how you evaluated and selected.
5. **Missing dates**: Every post needs a publish date and ideally a "last updated" date.
6. **Over-claiming**: Don't claim to be "the definitive guide" unless it genuinely is. Overpromising erodes trust.
