# E-E-A-T Signals (EA-1 to EA-7)

## EA-1: Author Attribution

**What it is:** Blog posts and content pages include author bylines, link to author bio pages, and display credentials or expertise signals for each author.

**Recommendation:** Add author bylines to every blog post and content page. Link each byline to a dedicated author page that includes the author's photo, bio, credentials, relevant experience, and links to their other content. Include Author schema markup (JSON-LD) on author pages. For YMYL topics, author expertise signals are especially critical.

**Why it matters:** Google's Quality Rater Guidelines explicitly evaluate author expertise as part of E-E-A-T assessment. Content with clear author attribution signals first-hand experience and expertise. Anonymous content — especially on advice, how-to, or opinion topics — is rated lower by quality raters because there's no way to assess whether the author has relevant expertise.

**Key stat:** 78% of Google's Quality Rater Guidelines reference author or creator expertise as an evaluation criterion.

**Source:** Google. "Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines" — Section 3.2: Expertise, Experience, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness (E-E-A-T) (December 2024). Also: Google Search Central. "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content." Google Developers Documentation (2024).

**PASS criteria:**
- Blog posts include author bylines with the author's name
- Author names link to a dedicated author page or bio section
- Author pages include credentials, photo, or relevant experience
- Author schema markup present on author pages

**FAIL criteria:**
- Blog posts published without author attribution
- Author names present but no linked bio or credentials
- Generic attribution ("Admin", "Team", "Staff Writer") without individual identity
- No author pages or bio sections anywhere on the site

## EA-2: About Page Depth

**What it is:** The site has a substantive About page that establishes the team's expertise, company story, mission, and relevant credentials — not just a placeholder paragraph.

**Recommendation:** Build an About page that goes beyond a generic mission statement. Include the founding story and what problem the team set out to solve. Showcase team members with photos, titles, and relevant expertise. Highlight industry experience, certifications, awards, or speaking engagements. Link to any media mentions, publications, or external validation.

**Why it matters:** The About page is a primary trust signal for both users and search quality raters. Google's Quality Rater Guidelines specifically instruct raters to check the About page to evaluate the website's authoritativeness and trustworthiness. A thin or missing About page raises red flags about the organization behind the content.

**Key stat:** Quality raters are explicitly instructed to check About pages when evaluating E-E-A-T.

**Source:** Google. "Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines" — Section 2.5.3: Finding About Us and Contact Information (December 2024). Also: Google Search Central. "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content." Google Developers Documentation (2024).

**PASS criteria:**
- About page exists and is linked from main navigation or footer
- Includes founding story or company origin narrative
- Team members listed with photos, titles, and relevant credentials
- Industry expertise, certifications, or notable achievements mentioned

**FAIL criteria:**
- No About page, or About page is a single paragraph
- Generic "we're passionate about X" without specifics
- No team members, photos, or individual credentials shown
- About page missing from navigation (hard to find)

## EA-3: Original Research & Proprietary Data

**What it is:** The site publishes original research, proprietary data, unique analyses, or first-hand case studies — content that cannot be found anywhere else on the web.

**Recommendation:** Publish at least some content based on original research, proprietary data, or unique first-hand experience. This can include internal surveys, product usage data (anonymized), industry benchmarks you've compiled, original experiments, or detailed case studies with specific metrics. Reference this original content from other articles to establish topical authority.

**Why it matters:** Google's Helpful Content system and E-E-A-T framework reward content that demonstrates first-hand experience and adds unique value. Sites that only repackage information available elsewhere provide no incremental value to searchers. Original research is the strongest form of "Experience" in E-E-A-T and the most linkable content type — it naturally attracts backlinks.

**Key stat:** Content with original research receives 6x more backlinks than content without, according to Backlinko's analysis of 912 million blog posts.

**Source:** Google Search Central. "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content" — specifically the "Experience" element of E-E-A-T (2024). Also: Backlinko. "We Analyzed 912 Million Blog Posts. Here's What We Learned About Content Marketing" (2023). Supplemented by: Google. "Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines" — Section 3.2 on Experience (December 2024).

**PASS criteria:**
- At least one piece of content based on original research, proprietary data, or unique analysis
- Case studies with specific metrics, outcomes, or named clients
- Content that clearly demonstrates first-hand experience with the topic
- Data visualizations, charts, or findings not available elsewhere

**FAIL criteria:**
- All content is generic advice available on dozens of other sites
- No case studies, original data, or unique analyses published
- Blog relies entirely on secondary sources without adding original perspective
- Content reads as AI-generated summaries of existing information

## EA-4: External Citations

**What it is:** Content pages link out to authoritative external sources — research papers, government data, industry reports, or established publications — to support claims and provide additional context.

**Recommendation:** Include outbound links to authoritative sources in your content. Cite research papers, government statistics, industry reports, or established publications when making claims. Use 2-5 external citations per substantive content page. Link to primary sources rather than secondary aggregators. Never make statistical claims without a source link.

**Why it matters:** Outbound links to authoritative sources are a trust signal that demonstrates content rigor. Google's Quality Rater Guidelines instruct raters to verify whether claims are supported by external evidence. Content that makes claims without citations appears less trustworthy. Linking out also contextualizes your content within the broader knowledge graph.

**Key stat:** Pages with outbound links to authoritative domains correlate with higher trust scores in quality rater evaluations.

**Source:** Google. "Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines" — Section 3.2: Trustworthiness (December 2024). Also: Reboot Online. "Outgoing Links Study: Impact of External Links on Rankings" (2023). Supplemented by: Moz. "External Links" — Moz SEO Learning Center (2023).

**PASS criteria:**
- Blog posts include outbound links to authoritative external sources
- Statistical claims are backed by source citations
- Links point to primary sources (research, official docs) not just other blog posts
- At least 2-3 external citations per substantive content page

**FAIL criteria:**
- No outbound links in blog content (internal links only)
- Statistical claims made without any source citation
- External links only to the company's own properties
- Links to low-quality or irrelevant external sources

## EA-5: Trust Signals

**What it is:** The site includes fundamental trust infrastructure: privacy policy, terms of service, accessible contact information, and where applicable, security certifications or compliance badges.

**Recommendation:** Include a privacy policy and terms of service linked from the footer. Provide accessible contact information (email, phone, or contact form) — not hidden behind multiple clicks. Display security certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001), compliance badges (GDPR, HIPAA), or payment security indicators where relevant. Add a physical address if the company has one.

**Why it matters:** Google's Quality Rater Guidelines explicitly instruct raters to check for contact information and trust-related pages. Missing privacy policies, hidden contact info, or absent terms of service are signals of a low-trust website. For SaaS companies handling customer data, security certifications provide concrete trust evidence beyond marketing claims.

**Key stat:** Quality raters are instructed to look for contact information, privacy policies, and trust signals as baseline trust indicators.

**Source:** Google. "Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines" — Section 2.5.3: Finding About Us, Contact Information, and Customer Service Information (December 2024). Also: Google Search Central. "Build and submit a sitemap — prerequisites include having a clear site structure with trust pages" (2024).

**PASS criteria:**
- Privacy policy and terms of service linked from footer or main navigation
- Contact information easily accessible (within 2 clicks from any page)
- Security certifications or compliance badges displayed where relevant (SOC 2, GDPR, etc.)
- Physical address or registered business information available

**FAIL criteria:**
- No privacy policy or terms of service
- Contact information hidden, missing, or only available after login
- SaaS handling sensitive data with no security certification mentions
- No physical address, company registration, or verifiable business identity

## EA-6: Case Studies & Customer Proof

**What it is:** The site features named case studies, detailed customer stories, or specific outcome data — not just logos or vague testimonials.

**Recommendation:** Publish at least 2-3 case studies with named customers, specific challenges, the solution implemented, and quantified outcomes (e.g., "reduced onboarding time by 40%"). Include customer quotes, company details, and relevant context. If customer NDAs prevent naming, use anonymized case studies with enough detail to be credible (industry, company size, specific metrics).

**Why it matters:** Case studies demonstrate first-hand "Experience" — the first E in E-E-A-T. They provide specific, verifiable evidence that the product delivers results. For B2B SaaS, case studies are also critical for buyer decision-making: 73% of B2B buyers consult case studies before purchasing. Detailed customer proof signals expertise and builds the kind of trust that Google's quality framework rewards.

**Key stat:** 73% of B2B buyers consult case studies during the purchasing process.

**Source:** Google. "Google Search Quality Rater Guidelines" — Section 3.2: Experience (December 2024). Also: Demand Gen Report. "2023 Content Preferences Survey" — B2B buyer content consumption patterns (2023). Supplemented by: Content Marketing Institute. "B2B Content Marketing Benchmarks, Budgets, and Trends" (2024).

**PASS criteria:**
- At least 2 named case studies with specific outcomes
- Quantified results (percentages, time saved, revenue impact)
- Customer quotes with name, title, and company
- Case studies linked from main navigation or prominently featured

**FAIL criteria:**
- No case studies or customer stories published
- Only logo walls without accompanying stories or outcomes
- Vague testimonials ("Great product!") without specifics
- Case studies hidden or not linked from main navigation

## EA-7: Content Freshness

**What it is:** Content pages display publish dates and update dates, and the site regularly publishes or updates content to signal ongoing relevance and active maintenance.

**Recommendation:** Display publish dates on all blog posts and content pages. When content is updated, show both the original publish date and the last-updated date. Regularly update high-performing content to keep it current. Publish new content on a consistent cadence. Remove or redirect outdated content that no longer provides value.

**Why it matters:** Google uses content freshness as a ranking signal, particularly for queries where recency matters (news, technology, trends). The "Query Deserves Freshness" (QDF) algorithm boosts recent content for time-sensitive topics. Stale content with outdated information erodes trust and E-E-A-T signals. Showing dates also helps users assess content relevance.

**Key stat:** Google's QDF algorithm applies to ~35% of queries, boosting fresher content for time-sensitive topics.

**Source:** Google Search Central. "Google Search Essentials — Key best practices" (2024). Also: Amit Singhal. "Google Search and Search Engine Optimization" — Official Google Blog, describing the freshness algorithm (2011, with ongoing updates). Supplemented by: Moz. "Google Freshness Update — What It Means and How to Adapt" (2023).

**PASS criteria:**
- Blog posts display publish dates visibly
- Updated content shows a "last updated" date
- Content published within the last 6 months exists on the blog
- No obviously outdated content with stale data or references (e.g., "in 2021, the latest trend is...")

**FAIL criteria:**
- No dates displayed on blog posts or content pages
- Most recent content is over 12 months old
- Content references outdated data, trends, or technologies without updates
- No indication of when content was published or last updated
