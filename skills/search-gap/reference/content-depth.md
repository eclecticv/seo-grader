# Content Depth (CD-1 to CD-6)

## CD-1: Content Depth Per Page

**What it is:** Key content pages (blog posts, guides, resource pages) have sufficient depth to thoroughly cover their topic — not thin pages with superficial treatment.

**Recommendation:** Ensure blog posts and content pages have enough depth to fully address the user's intent for that topic. For informational content, aim for 1,500+ words on substantive topics. Avoid thin pages under 300 words that add no unique value. Depth is not about word count alone — it's about comprehensively answering the question a visitor came with. Every section should add value; don't pad with filler.

**Why it matters:** Google's Helpful Content system evaluates whether content provides a satisfying experience for visitors. Thin pages that superficially skim a topic fail to satisfy search intent, leading to pogo-sticking (users clicking back to search results). Comprehensive content ranks better because it answers follow-up questions and keeps users on the page — strong engagement signals.

**Key stat:** The average first-page Google result contains 1,447 words. Content comprehensiveness correlates more strongly with rankings than any other on-page factor.

**Source:** Backlinko. "We Analyzed 11.8 Million Google Search Results. Here's What We Learned About SEO" (2023). Also: Google Search Central. "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content" (2024). Supplemented by: SEMrush. "State of Content Marketing 2023" — analysis of 500,000+ articles (2023).

**PASS criteria:**
- Blog posts and guides average 1,000+ words on substantive topics
- No key content pages under 300 words (excluding landing pages and product pages)
- Content thoroughly addresses the topic without obvious gaps
- Depth is appropriate to the topic complexity (not padded with filler)

**FAIL criteria:**
- Multiple blog posts under 300 words on topics that warrant depth
- Content superficially covers topics without actionable detail
- Thin "stub" pages that exist for SEO but don't satisfy user intent
- Key topics spread across many short posts instead of comprehensive guides

## CD-2: Topic Coverage Breadth

**What it is:** The site has content covering its core topics comprehensively — the fundamental questions, concepts, and use cases that its target audience searches for.

**Recommendation:** Identify 5-7 core topics that your target audience cares about. Ensure you have at least one substantive piece of content for each core topic. Map content to the questions your audience asks at each stage of the buyer journey. Look for obvious gaps — topics where competitors have content but you don't.

**Why it matters:** Google evaluates topical authority — how comprehensively a site covers a subject area. Sites with deep coverage across related topics are perceived as more authoritative than sites with isolated, unrelated posts. If you're a project management tool but have no content about task prioritization, sprint planning, or team collaboration, you're leaving topical authority on the table.

**Key stat:** Sites with topical authority (comprehensive coverage of a subject) rank 30-50% higher for related queries than sites with isolated content.

**Source:** Google Search Central. "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content" — particularly the guidance on demonstrating expertise across a topic area (2024). Also: Ahrefs. "Topical Authority: What It Is and How to Build It" (2023). Supplemented by: HubSpot. "How Topic Clusters and Pillar Pages Changed Our SEO Strategy" (2023).

**PASS criteria:**
- Content exists for each core topic relevant to the company's positioning
- Key audience questions are addressed across the content library
- No obvious gaps in topic coverage compared to the site's stated expertise
- Content covers multiple angles of core topics (how-to, comparison, strategy)

**FAIL criteria:**
- Major core topics have no dedicated content
- Content covers only 1-2 topics while ignoring other core areas
- Blog topics are random and don't connect to the company's positioning
- Obvious audience questions unanswered anywhere on the site

## CD-3: FAQ & Educational Content

**What it is:** The site addresses common questions through FAQ sections, how-to guides, or educational content that maps to "People Also Ask" queries in search results.

**Recommendation:** Add FAQ sections to key product and feature pages. Create educational content that answers the questions your audience types into Google. Check "People Also Ask" boxes in search results for your core topics and ensure you've addressed those questions. Use FAQPage schema markup to enable FAQ rich results in SERPs.

**Why it matters:** "People Also Ask" (PAA) boxes appear in over 65% of Google searches. FAQ content that directly answers these questions has a strong chance of being featured in PAA results or as a featured snippet. FAQ content also serves the "information" stage of the buyer journey — building trust and awareness before the visitor is ready to evaluate solutions.

**Key stat:** "People Also Ask" boxes appear in over 65% of Google search results. FAQ-structured content is 2x more likely to appear as a featured snippet.

**Source:** SEMrush. "People Also Ask Study: Analysis of 1 Million SERPs" (2023). Also: Google Search Central. "Featured snippets and your website" (2024). Supplemented by: Ahrefs. "People Also Ask: What, Why, and How to Target Them" (2023).

**PASS criteria:**
- FAQ sections present on product or feature pages
- Educational how-to content published for core topics
- Content addresses questions that appear in "People Also Ask" for target queries
- FAQPage schema markup used on pages with FAQ sections

**FAIL criteria:**
- No FAQ sections on any product or feature pages
- No educational or how-to content in the content library
- Obvious "People Also Ask" questions for core topics left unanswered
- FAQ content exists but lacks schema markup (missing rich result opportunity)

## CD-4: Visual Content Quality

**What it is:** Content pages include relevant visual elements — diagrams, charts, screenshots, infographics, or videos — that enhance understanding and break up text walls.

**Recommendation:** Add relevant visuals to substantive content pages. Use product screenshots to illustrate features. Create diagrams or flowcharts for process explanations. Include data visualizations for any statistics or comparisons. Custom visuals (not stock photos) add more value. Every visual should serve a purpose — illustrating a concept, showing a process, or presenting data.

**Why it matters:** Content with relevant images receives 94% more total views than text-only content. Visuals improve content comprehension, increase time on page, and reduce bounce rates — all engagement signals that influence search rankings. Google Image Search is also a significant traffic channel. Pages with only dense text blocks have higher abandonment rates.

**Key stat:** Content with relevant images gets 94% more total views than text-only content. Posts with images every 75-100 words receive 2x the shares.

**Source:** Backlinko. "We Analyzed 912 Million Blog Posts. Here's What We Learned About Content Marketing" (2023). Also: BuzzSumo & OkDork. "How to Go Viral: The Content Analysis Study" (2023). Supplemented by: HubSpot. "The Ultimate List of Marketing Statistics" — image engagement data (2024).

**PASS criteria:**
- Blog posts include relevant visuals (screenshots, diagrams, charts) — not just stock photos
- At least one meaningful visual per 500 words of content on average
- Product screenshots or demos used to illustrate features
- Custom visuals preferred over generic stock imagery

**FAIL criteria:**
- Blog posts are pure text walls with no visual elements
- Only generic stock photos used (people shaking hands, abstract shapes)
- Feature pages describe functionality without any screenshots or demos
- Data-heavy content presented without charts or visualizations

## CD-5: Content Architecture (Pillar/Cluster)

**What it is:** The site organizes content in a pillar-cluster structure — comprehensive pillar pages covering broad topics, linked to cluster content that dives deep into specific subtopics.

**Recommendation:** Organize content into 3-5 topic clusters. Create a comprehensive pillar page (2,000+ words) for each cluster's core topic. Write cluster content (blog posts, guides) that covers specific subtopics in depth. Interlink pillar pages to their cluster content and vice versa. This creates a clear topical architecture that signals expertise to both users and search engines.

**Why it matters:** The pillar-cluster model aligns with how Google evaluates topical authority. A well-linked cluster of content on a topic signals deep expertise. HubSpot's internal study showed that adopting pillar-cluster architecture increased their blog's organic traffic by 13% in 3 months. The structure also improves internal linking naturally, distributing page authority throughout related content.

**Key stat:** HubSpot's pillar-cluster adoption increased organic search traffic by 13% in 3 months. Topic clusters improve rankings for all pages in the cluster, not just the pillar.

**Source:** HubSpot. "Topic Clusters: The Next Evolution of SEO" — internal research and results (2023). Also: Ahrefs. "Content Hubs: How to Plan, Create, and Optimize Them" (2023). Supplemented by: Google Search Central. "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content" — guidance on demonstrating topical depth (2024).

**PASS criteria:**
- At least one identifiable content cluster (pillar + 3+ supporting articles)
- Pillar pages comprehensively cover broad topics with links to subtopic content
- Cluster content links back to the pillar page
- Internal linking between related content is contextual and purposeful

**FAIL criteria:**
- Blog posts are isolated — no topical grouping or content architecture visible
- No comprehensive pillar pages for core topics
- Content covers diverse topics with no interconnection or structure
- No visible content strategy — blog reads as a random collection of posts

## CD-6: Funnel Content Balance (TOFU/MOFU/BOFU)

**What it is:** The content library covers all stages of the buyer journey — top-of-funnel awareness content, middle-of-funnel consideration content, and bottom-of-funnel decision content.

**Recommendation:** Map content to the three buyer journey stages. TOFU (awareness): educational guides, industry trends, problem-awareness content. MOFU (consideration): comparison guides, how-to content, use case deep-dives. BOFU (decision): case studies, ROI calculators, product demos, pricing guides, competitor comparisons. Most SaaS blogs over-index on TOFU and neglect MOFU/BOFU content.

**Why it matters:** Visitors arrive at different stages of the buying process. TOFU content attracts traffic but doesn't convert. BOFU content converts but has limited search volume. A balanced funnel ensures you capture visitors at every stage and nurture them toward conversion. Sites missing MOFU/BOFU content lose visitors who've moved past awareness — they leave to find evaluation content on competitor sites.

**Key stat:** Only 22% of B2B companies rate their content marketing as successful at nurturing leads across funnel stages. Companies with balanced funnel content see 67% more leads than those focused only on TOFU.

**Source:** Content Marketing Institute. "B2B Content Marketing Benchmarks, Budgets, and Trends" (2024). Also: HubSpot. "The Ultimate Guide to Content Marketing in 2024" (2024). Supplemented by: Demand Gen Report. "2023 Content Preferences Survey" — B2B buyer content consumption patterns (2023).

**PASS criteria:**
- Content exists for awareness (educational, industry, problem-framing)
- Content exists for consideration (comparisons, how-to, use cases)
- Content exists for decision (case studies, ROI, demos, pricing guides)
- Reasonable balance across stages — not heavily skewed to one funnel stage

**FAIL criteria:**
- Content is almost entirely TOFU (generic blog posts) with no MOFU/BOFU
- No comparison content, use case guides, or evaluation resources
- No case studies, ROI content, or buying guides for decision-stage visitors
- Content library serves only one stage of the buyer journey
